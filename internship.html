Internship & Training

A record of practical training — the DBT Builder Lab internship and apiculture (beekeeping) training — and the skills gained from each.

🧪 Internship

DBT Builder Lab Internship

An internship at a DBT (Department of Biotechnology) Builder Lab focused on hands-on exposure to modern plant biology techniques, supporting the bridge between classroom Botany and applied biotechnology research.

Typical activities in this kind of internship include plant tissue culture (sterile technique, media preparation, sub-culturing), DNA extraction and basic molecular biology protocols, microscopy and sample preparation, lab safety and documentation practices, and working with research mentors on small assigned projects.

🧬 Skills Gained

Practical Skills — Lab

  • Aseptic technique and sterile media preparation
  • Plant tissue culture and sub-culturing
  • Basic DNA extraction protocols
  • Microscopy and slide preparation
  • Lab documentation and data recording
  • Working collaboratively on research tasks
🐝 Skills Gained

Practical Skills — Apiculture

  • Hive setup and maintenance
  • Bee colony inspection and health monitoring
  • Honey extraction and processing
  • Identifying bee diseases and pests
  • Role of bees in pollination and crop yield
  • Basic queen rearing concepts
🐝 Training

Apiculture (Beekeeping) Training

Apiculture training covers the practical management of honeybee colonies for honey production and crop pollination. Training typically includes hands-on hive inspection, understanding colony structure (queen, workers, drones), seasonal management practices, and safe handling techniques.

Beekeeping is closely linked to agriculture and botany — bees are critical pollinators for many crops and wild plant species, making apiculture knowledge valuable for both farming systems and biodiversity conservation.

📝 Reflection

Why These Experiences Matter

Combining lab-based biotechnology training with field-based apiculture and agriculture practice gives a well-rounded foundation — bridging molecular plant science with practical, ecosystem-level understanding. This combination is valuable for research roles, agricultural extension work, and conservation-focused careers alike.
